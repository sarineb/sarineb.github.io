Predicting movement variability using machine learning with brain signals

See the paper I published here.

Aim

Using machine learning to find out if the brain is responsible for the variability observed in movement details.

Background

  • Human movements vary in their details even when repeated exactly (goal-equivalent variability).
  • The mechanism of goal-equivalent variability in the nervous system is unknown.
  • Hypothesis: If goal-equivalent variability is produced at the brain level, the upcoming repetition class can be accurately predicted using EEG brain signals.

Experimental data

EEG and fingertip force data were obtained while healthy participants performed a bimanual, two-finger force matching task. Repetitions of the same task could be implemented using either right>left or left>right forces. Our dataset consisted of EEG signals right before movement onset, as well as true force labels determined from the fingertip force sensors (right>left or left>right).

EEG Prediction

Our dataset consisted of EEG signals right before movement onset, as well as true force labels determined using the fingertip force sensors (right>left or left>right). We used Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA) with EEG preprocessing using Common Spatial Patterns (CSP) to predict if the upcoming repetition belonged to right>left or left>right (with leave-one-out cross-validation).

Results

Below are the prediction results compared with the true labels for all subjects (from a linear mixed-effects model to account for random effects for participant), as well as the most important features for prediction displayed on a head map of brain activity. An ROC curve averaged across all participants resulted in a 72% AUC.

a. EEG-predicted classes versus force-based classes of all 18 participants. For each participant, the classifier outputs of repetitions from each class (FL > FR and FR > FL) were averaged and joined by a line. Most participants show a clear distinction between the two classes as indicated by a positive slope of the line. Linear mixed-effect modeling demonstrated a significant effect of force-based repetition class on EEG-based classifier output (p < 0.0001, ****); b. the most important and second most important common spatial patterns (CSP) used for the EEG-based classification of repetitions where FL > FR (left) and repetitions where FR > FL (right), averaged across 18 participants. Enhanced EEG amplitudes are seen in the left hand motor area when FL leads (top left), and vice-versa when FR leads.

Individual ROC curves of all participants and overall ROC curve across all participants. The colored lines represent each individual participant’s ROC curve derived from the normalized outputs of all leave-one-out cross-validated classifiers. The bold black curve is the overall ROC across all participants, and is determined using all participants’ normalized classifier outputs. The area under the curve (AUC) of the overall ROC curve is 0.72.
